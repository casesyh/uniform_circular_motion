Uniform circular motion: the motion of an infinite number of circles
Yuhao

Intro

  • Along with the uniform motion, the uniform circular motion is one of the most amazing motions of all the motions.
  • The uniform motion is a motion of one straight line.
  • In contrast, the uniform circular motion is a motion of an infinite number of circles.

The infinite number of circles

The first circle

  • Suppose an object is moving along a circle at a constant speed (by speed we mean the magnitude of the velocity, which includes not only the magnitude but also the direction)
  • In this case, we say the object is in uniform circular motion
  • The trajectory of the object forms the first circle

  • To calculate the magnitude of the velocity, we divide the circumference of the first circle by the time it takes the object to complete one cycle of the circular motion

    \begin{equation} \label{org2454b7b} v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} \end{equation}

The second circle

  • The direction of the object's velocity is along the tangential at each point on the first circle
  • If we move the tail of the velocity vector to the origin, the head of the velocity vector traces a circle
  • This is the second circle
  • The acceleration is the instaneous change of the velocity
  • The magnitude of the acceleration is constant, since the motion is uniform
  • To calculate the magnitude of the acceleration, we divide the circumference of the second circle by the time it takes the object to complete one cycle of the circular motion
\begin{equation} \label{org79ab77e} a_c = \frac{2\pi v}{T} \end{equation}

Other forms of the formula

  • If we plug in \(v\) from Eq.\(~\)\eqref{org2454b7b} to Eq.\(~\)\eqref{org79ab77e}

    \begin{equation} a_c = \frac{2\pi v}{T} = \frac{2\pi \frac{2\pi r} {T}}{T} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2} \end{equation}
  • From Eq.\(~\)\eqref{org2454b7b}, \(T = 2\pi r/ v\)

  • Plugging this in Eq.\(~\)\eqref{org79ab77e}, we have

    \begin{equation} a_c = \frac{2\pi v}{T} = \frac{2\pi v}{\left(\frac{2\pi r}{v}\right)}{T} = \frac{v^2}{r} \end{equation}

The thrid circle

  • The direction of the acceleration of the object is along the tangential fo the second circle at each point on the second circle
  • If we move the tail of the acceleration vector to the origin, the head of the velocity vector traces a circle
  • This is the third circle
  • The acceleration of the acceleration, which we may call \(\overrightarrow{b_c}\), is the instantaneous change of the acceleration
  • The magnitude \(b_c\) is constant since the motion is uniform

  • To calcualte \(b_c\), we devide the circumference of the second circle by the time it takes the object to complete one cycle of the circular motion

    \begin{equation} \label{org8f07024} b_c = \frac{2\pi a_c}{T} \end{equation}

An infinite number of circles

  • When we keep on with the process we get an infinite number of circles
  • The acceleration of the acceleration of the acceleration … can be calcualted

  • The expresion for the nth one \(a_{cn}\) would be

    \begin{equation} \label{orgb9ce6f0} a_{cn} = \frac{(2\pi)^n r}{T^n} \end{equation}
